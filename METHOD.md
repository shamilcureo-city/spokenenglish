# Speakwell — the method (the learning science behind it)

## The one main conceptual learning

> **Speaking fluency is a SKILL of automaticity, not a stock of knowledge.
> You learn to speak by speaking — so the method is maximal, meaningful, low-anxiety talking time,
> with feedback that builds confidence instead of breaking flow.**

Our users (Tier-2/3 Indians who read & write English but freeze) **already have the knowledge** —
grammar, vocabulary, comprehension. They freeze for two reasons, and neither is "they don't know
English":

1. **It isn't automatic.** Their English is *declarative* knowledge (rules they can recall slowly),
   not *procedural/automatic* skill. Under real-time pressure they "translate in their head" because
   retrieval is effortful, not reflexive.
2. **Anxiety blocks it.** Fear of judgement raises the **affective filter** — even known words won't
   come out.

So Speakwell's job is **not to teach English** — it's to **automatize what they already have** and
**turn the anxiety down**. That single idea produces five design rules (the DNA):

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| **Output > input** — the learner talks most | You automatize a skill by *doing* it, not hearing about it |
| **Meaning > form** — real communication, not drills | Fluency comes from using language to *do things*, not from correctness exercises |
| **Reps build reflex** — same functions, spaced & varied | Automaticity = repeated retrieval until it's fast and effortless |
| **Affective filter down** — safe, no shame, mistakes are the point | Anxiety is the gate; lower it and the knowledge flows |
| **Feedback after, not during** — gentle, in the mother tongue | Correcting mid-flow spikes anxiety and kills fluency; delayed recasts let them notice without freezing |

---

## The second engine: the habit loop

The method above only works if it actually *happens*. Automaticity and desensitisation need **daily
reps** — and a 5-minute skill dies the moment the learner stops coming back. So Speakwell runs **two
interlocking loops**:

- **LEARNING loop** (inside one 5-min session): *Learn the chunks → Speak live → mother-tongue recap →
  a bit more automatic* ↺
- **HABIT loop** (across days): *Cue → tiny Action → Variable Reward → Investment → a stronger Cue
  tomorrow* ↺

> **The learning loop builds the skill in a session; the habit loop delivers the reps the skill needs
> across days.** Most language apps have one and not the other. Speakwell needs both — and the habit
> loop is also the #1 retention/growth asset (see [MASTERPLAN.md](MASTERPLAN.md)).

The guiding rule is **behaviour, not willpower** — Fogg's **B = MAP**: a behaviour fires only when
**M**otivation, **A**bility and a **P**rompt converge. Motivation is unreliable, so we win on
**Ability** (make it tiny + frictionless) and **Prompt** (a reliable cue), and we use **emotion**
(immediate celebration) to wire it in.

## All the methods (and how Speakwell uses each)

Grouped by the job each does. Each is an established second-language-acquisition (SLA) principle.

### A. Input — what they're exposed to
- **Comprehensible Input / i+1 (Krashen).** Language *slightly above* current level — understandable
  but stretching. → The tutor pitches difficulty just above the learner's placement level.
- **Scaffolded / modified input.** Simplify and support, then fade. → Lessons start with key phrases,
  then withdraw the prop.

### B. Output — the core (speaking)
- **Output Hypothesis / pushed output (Swain).** Being *forced to produce* makes you notice gaps and
  proceduralize. → Every lesson is a live talk where the learner must speak.
- **Maximal talk time.** The learner should speak the majority of the session. → Tutor turns are
  short (1–3 sentences); it asks one thing and listens.
- **Fluency practice (meaning-focused).** Speak for *getting the message across*, not perfection. →
  The daily free-talk + the "Speak" step never correct mid-flow.

### C. Interaction — conversation as the engine
- **Interaction Hypothesis (Long).** Meaning *negotiated* in real conversation drives acquisition. →
  Unscripted two-way voice, with clarification and follow-ups.
- **Communicative Language Teaching (CLT).** Language = communication; organise by **functions** (what
  you *do*: introduce, disagree, persuade), not grammar. → The whole syllabus is functional.
- **Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT).** Do a real task *in* English (order food, run a meeting). →
  Each lesson is a real situational task.
- **Recasts (implicit feedback).** The tutor reformulates correctly *without stopping* the learner. →
  The partner models good English naturally instead of "correcting."

### D. Automaticity — turning knowledge into reflex
- **Skill Acquisition Theory / proceduralization (DeKeyser; Anderson's ACT-R).** Declarative →
  procedural → automatic through practice. → This is the whole point of speaking every lesson.
- **Deliberate practice (Ericsson).** Targeted, repeated, with feedback. → Lessons target specific
  moves; redo-to-improve.
- **Repetition with variation / the 4-3-2 technique.** Re-do the same talk in shrinking time → fluency
  jumps. → Redo a lesson; warm-ups recycle evergreen functions.
- **Lexical Approach / formulaic chunks (Lewis; Nation).** Learn phrases as *single units* ("I'm not
  so sure about that") — far faster to retrieve than building word-by-word. → Each lesson's "Say it
  like this" is 5–8 ready chunks.
- **Shadowing.** Imitate a model's rhythm/prosody. → (Roadmap) repeat-after for pronunciation.

### E. Confidence — lowering the affective filter
- **Affective Filter Hypothesis (Krashen).** Low anxiety = acquisition; high anxiety = block. →
  Judgement-free, no mid-talk correction, "mistakes are the point."
- **Willingness to Communicate (MacIntyre).** Build the *disposition* to speak. → Tiny low-stakes
  daily wins grow the willingness.
- **Desensitisation / little-and-often.** Small repeated low-stakes exposure dampens the anxiety
  response faster than rare intense sessions. → 5 minutes a day.

### F. Feedback & correction — the delicate part
- **Focus on Form, not Focus on FormS (Long).** Brief attention to form *inside* meaningful activity
  — never isolated grammar drills. → The recap, not the conversation.
- **Corrective-feedback types** — *recast* (implicit), *prompt/elicitation*, *explicit*,
  *metalinguistic*. → Recasts live; explicit + metalinguistic fixes in the recap.
- **Delayed feedback.** After the talk, so it protects fluency and lowers anxiety. → The confidence-
  first recap is post-session by design.
- **Noticing (Schmidt).** You only acquire what you *notice*. → The recap quotes "what you said → a
  stronger way," making the gap visible.
- **Contrastive analysis / L1 transfer (mother-tongue scaffolding).** Explain the error via its
  *mother-tongue cause* (the MTI fix) in the learner's language — judicious L1 use accelerates
  noticing. → Recap fixes are explained in Hindi/Tamil/etc.

### G. Scheduling & retention
- **Spaced / distributed practice.** Space reviews over days, not cram. → (Roadmap) resurface weak
  functions; daily habit.
- **Interleaving.** Mix functions rather than blocking one. → The course rotates functions across units.
- **Habit (consistency > intensity).** Motor skills + desensitisation need frequency. → The 5-min/day
  streak loop.

### H. Structure & progression
- **Scaffolding & the Zone of Proximal Development (Vygotsky).** Support just above current ability,
  then fade. → Learn → Speak → Feedback, props withdrawn over a unit.
- **Functional/communicative syllabus + CEFR "can-do" descriptors.** Measurable communicative goals
  ("I can disagree politely"), not grammar coverage. → Every lesson has a can-do.
- **Placement to i+1.** Start each learner at *their* stretch zone. → The 2-min spoken placement.

### I. Pronunciation / intelligibility
- **Intelligibility over native-accent (Jenkins — Lingua Franca Core).** Goal = *be understood*, not
  sound American/British. → Pronunciation feedback is framed as clarity, never accent-shaming.
- **Prosody & high-yield contrasts.** For Indian English, stress-timing + a few key sounds (/v/–/w/,
  /θ/, retroflex t/d) carry most intelligibility. → (Roadmap) targeted pronunciation notes.

### J. Motivation & metacognition
- **Self-efficacy (Bandura).** Belief you *can* drives effort. → Visible progress, stars, "you did it."
- **Relevance / autonomy.** Practise what matters to *you* (jobs, real situations). → Job/status-
  framed scenarios and topics.

### K. Habit formation & behaviour design — the daily engine
- **The Habit Loop — Cue → Routine → Reward (Duhigg).** A craving for the reward wires the routine to
  the cue. → A daily cue (reminder + the home-screen streak) → the 5-min talk → a satisfying reward.
- **The 4 Laws of Behaviour Change (Clear, *Atomic Habits*): make it Obvious · Attractive · Easy ·
  Satisfying.** → Obvious (set a daily time + visible streak); Attractive (job/status framing, varied
  praise); Easy (one tap, <90s, no signup); Satisfying (instant stars/XP/streak).
- **B = MAP (Fogg).** Behaviour = Motivation × Ability × Prompt; don't bet on motivation — make it
  **tiny** and **prompt** it. → "Just one conversation"; the 2-minute entry.
- **The Hook Model (Eyal): Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment.** Variable rewards keep it
  fresh; investment stores value that *loads the next trigger*. → The unscripted talk is inherently a
  variable reward; the streak/XP/progress/clip is the investment.
- **Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer): "I'll practise at [time], in [place]."** Specifying
  when/where hugely lifts follow-through. → Onboarding asks "when are your 5 free minutes?" and sets
  the reminder to it.
- **Habit stacking / anchoring.** Attach the new habit to an existing routine. → "After your morning
  chai, do your 5-minute English" (suggested anchors at onboarding).
- **The 2-minute rule / tiny start.** Make *starting* trivially easy. → Open → talking in <90s; even
  one sentence counts as a "done" day.
- **Identity-based habits.** Shift from "I'm *trying* to learn English" to **"I'm someone who speaks
  English every day."** The most durable change. → "Day 12 — you're becoming a daily speaker."
- **Variable reward / dopamine.** Unpredictable rewards are stickier than fixed ones. → Surprise "you
  nailed it!", varied recap praise, occasional bonus moments.
- **Streaks + loss aversion — but HUMANE.** The streak is stored value you won't want to break; but
  *punitive* streaks backfire (when one breaks, users quit all at once). → Free weekly
  **streak-freeze + repair**, **never-miss-twice**, warm copy, **no public shaming**.
- **Fresh-start effect & social accountability.** A new week/Monday is a natural restart; a partner who
  practises with you raises commitment. → The weekly goal + the "practise-with-me" invite.

## The Speakwell "method stack" in one paragraph

Live unscripted AI conversation (**output + interaction + i+1 + recasts**) × a confidence-first,
mother-tongue, *post-session* recap (**focus-on-form + contrastive analysis + noticing**, affective
filter down) × 5 minutes a day (**distributed practice + desensitisation + habit**) × a leveled
**functional/communicative** syllabus with **can-do** goals (CLT + scaffolding) × 5–8 ready-made
**chunks** per lesson (lexical approach → proceduralization) × placement to each learner's stretch
zone — all framed around **building the disposition and automaticity to speak**, not teaching English
they already know.

> **The honest "why it works":** we don't add knowledge — we **automatize the knowledge you have and
> remove the fear that blocks it**, by making you speak a little every day in a place where it's safe
> to be wrong.
